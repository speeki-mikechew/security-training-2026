Phishing & Social Engineering

What is it?

Phishing is the number one way attackers breach organisations. They send fake emails, Teams messages, or phone calls that look legitimate — tricking you into clicking links, sharing passwords, or transferring money. Social engineering goes further: attackers may impersonate a colleague, vendor, or executive to manipulate you into taking action.

Red flags to watch for
Urgency or pressure — "Act now or your account will be locked!" Legitimate companies rarely create panic.
Suspicious sender addresses — Hover over the "From" field in Outlook. Look for misspellings like support@speeki-security.com instead of support@speeki.com.
Strange links or attachments — Hover before clicking. If the URL doesn't match what's displayed, don't click it.
Unusual requests — Asking for passwords, wire transfers, or sensitive data over email or Teams. Always verify through a separate channel.
Scenario
Example: A fake IT Support email using Speeki branding. Notice the suspicious URL "speeki-login.com" when hovering over the button.
Example Attack

You get an email from "IT Support" saying your Microsoft 365 password expires in 1 hour. The link goes to speeki-login.com (not our real domain). The email uses the Speeki logo and looks professional. This is a phishing attempt — always check the actual URL and verify with IT through a separate Teams message or phone call.

Remember: STOP, VERIFY, REPORT

STOP — Don't click, reply, or download anything suspicious.
VERIFY — Contact the sender through a separate, trusted channel (Teams direct message, phone call).
REPORT — Forward the suspicious email to the IT/security team immediately. Better a false alarm than a missed attack.

Passwords & Access Control

Password requirements at Speeki
Weak vs strong passwords — and the minimum requirements for standard and admin accounts.

Strong passwords are your first line of defence. Speeki has different requirements based on your account type:

Standard user accounts — Minimum 8 characters. This applies to your Microsoft 365 account, cloud applications, and day-to-day logins. Mix letters, numbers, and special characters.
Admin and local admin accounts — Minimum 12 characters. This applies to local computer admin passwords, server access, or any account with elevated privileges. Use a passphrase like "BlueTiger$Dances99" for easier memorisation.
Best practices for everyone
Unique per account — Never reuse passwords. If one service is breached, attackers try your credentials everywhere (this is called credential stuffing).
Use a password manager — It generates and stores unique passwords for every account. You only need to remember one master password.
Enable MFA everywhere — Multi-factor authentication adds a second layer: your password + a code from Microsoft Authenticator. This is mandatory for all Speeki accounts.
Access control
Least Privilege Principle

Only request access to the systems and data you actually need for your role. If you change roles or no longer need access to a system, notify IT so permissions can be revoked. Never share your credentials with anyone — including your manager.

Key Takeaway

8-character minimum for standard accounts, 12-character minimum for admin accounts. Use unique passwords, a password manager, and always enable MFA.

Data Classification & Handling

Why it matters

Not all data is equal. Treating everything the same puts sensitive information at risk. Speeki classifies data into levels so everyone knows how to handle it properly.

Classification levels

Speeki uses Microsoft Purview sensitivity labels to classify all files, emails, and meeting content. These labels are applied automatically or manually and add footers to your documents. There are four levels:

Microsoft Purview in action — sensitivity labels in Word with the auto-labeling notification and document footer.
Restricted — Highly sensitive, business-critical, or legally regulated information. Access limited to executives or authorised personnel only. Examples: draft contracts, payroll data, board documents, source code, encryption keys. Footer: RESTRICTED
Confidential — Sensitive business or personal information needed for operations. Access restricted to specific roles with a business need. Examples: signed contracts, customer PII, HR reviews, financial reports. Footer: CONFIDENTIAL
Internal — Company-only information used in daily operations. Not for public or external sharing. Examples: meeting notes, internal policies, routine communications. Footer: INTERNAL
Public — Documents intended for public consumption which can be freely distributed outside Speeki. Examples: marketing materials, product descriptions, release notes, external-facing policies.
How Purview labels work
Auto-labelling is enabled

Microsoft Purview automatically detects and labels sensitive content in your files and emails. You'll see a sensitivity label bar at the top of your documents in Office apps. You can also set labels manually. Labels apply across files, emails, and Teams meetings. When in doubt, apply a higher classification — it's easier to downgrade than to recover from a data leak.

Handling rules for remote workers
Daily Practices

Lock your screen when you step away — even at home (Win+L / Cmd+Ctrl+Q). Others may have access to your workspace.

File sharing: Use SharePoint and OneDrive only. Never email confidential data unencrypted or use personal cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox, etc.).

Cloud storage: All Speeki data must stay in Microsoft 365. Do not download confidential files to personal devices.

Disposal: Securely delete files you no longer need. When returning or replacing a device, ensure IT wipes it first.

Key Takeaway

Speeki uses four sensitivity levels: Public, Internal, Confidential, and Restricted. Labels are applied automatically by Microsoft Purview, but always check before sharing. When in doubt, classify higher. Use SharePoint/OneDrive only — never personal cloud storage.

Incident Reporting

What counts as an incident?
Example: A client report accidentally sent to the wrong external email — report immediately, don't try to fix it yourself.

A security incident is any event that threatens the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information. You don't need to be certain — if something feels wrong, report it.

Clicked a suspicious link or opened an unexpected attachment
Lost or stolen device (laptop, phone, USB drive)
Unauthorised person accessing systems or shared accounts
Data accidentally sent to the wrong person or shared publicly
Shared credentials or discovered exposed passwords
How to report
Reporting Steps

1. Notify your manager and the IT/security team immediately via Microsoft Teams or email.
2. Don't try to fix it yourself — preserve evidence (don't delete the suspicious email, don't wipe the device).
3. Document what happened: what, when, and how you noticed it.
4. Follow any additional instructions from the security team.

Working remotely

Since we're fully remote, physical security still applies to your home workspace. Keep your work devices secure, use a private workspace during calls discussing sensitive information, and never leave your laptop unattended in public spaces like cafes or co-working areas.

Key Takeaway

Report fast, report early. There is no penalty for false alarms — but delayed reporting can make a breach far worse. Every minute counts.

Safe Use of AI Tools at Work

AI is powerful — but risky if misused
Left: NEVER paste confidential data into public AI tools. Right: General, non-sensitive requests are fine.

AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, and Gemini are increasingly part of daily work. They can boost your productivity, but they also introduce new security risks. Anything you type into an AI tool could potentially be stored, used for model training, or exposed in a breach.

The golden rules
Never paste confidential data into public AI tools — No client names, financials, personal data, passwords, source code, or internal documents. Assume anything you type could become public.
Use only approved AI tools — Check with IT/management which AI tools are sanctioned for work use. Free consumer versions often have weaker data protections than enterprise plans.
Always review AI output — AI can generate plausible-sounding but incorrect information ("hallucinations"). Never blindly trust or copy-paste AI output into client deliverables without checking it.
Disclose AI usage where required — If you use AI to draft client deliverables, reports, or communications, follow Speeki's policy on disclosure.
Prompt injection — a new risk
What is Prompt Injection?

Attackers can hide malicious instructions inside documents, emails, or web pages. When you paste that content into an AI tool, the hidden instructions can trick the AI into leaking your data or performing harmful actions. Never paste untrusted content directly into AI prompts — especially full emails, web pages, or documents from unknown sources.

Key Takeaway

Treat AI tools like a public conversation. Never share anything you wouldn't post on a public bulletin board. Use approved tools, verify the output, and be aware of prompt injection risks.

AI-Powered Threats

Attackers use AI too
AI deepfakes can impersonate colleagues on video calls. Always verify unusual requests through a separate channel.

The same AI that helps you write emails and analyse data is now being used by attackers to create more convincing, harder-to-detect threats. Here's what to watch for.

New AI-powered attack types
AI-generated phishing — Attackers use AI to write flawless phishing emails with perfect grammar, no typos, and personalised details scraped from LinkedIn. The old "look for typos" advice is no longer enough.
Voice deepfakes — AI can clone someone's voice from just a few seconds of audio. Attackers have used fake CEO voice calls to authorise fraudulent wire transfers. If a voice call requests something unusual, hang up and verify through a separate channel.
Video deepfakes on calls — Real-time video deepfakes can impersonate colleagues on Teams calls. In one case, a company lost $25 million to a deepfake video call. If something feels off about a video call, verify identity through a separate channel.
Automated social engineering — AI chatbots can impersonate IT support or HR in real-time conversations via email or Teams messages, adapting their responses dynamically to sound convincing.
How to protect yourself
Defence Strategies

Verify through separate channels — Any unusual request (money, data, access) should be confirmed via a different communication method. If you got a Teams message, call them. If you got a call, message them on Teams.

Trust your instincts — If something feels off about a message, call, or video, pause and verify. AI-generated content can be very convincing but often has subtle inconsistencies.

Establish verification phrases — For high-stakes decisions (wire transfers, data access), consider having a pre-agreed verification phrase with your team.

Key Takeaway

AI makes attacks more convincing, not just more frequent. Your best defence remains the same: STOP, VERIFY through a separate channel, and REPORT. Technology changes; the human firewall remains critical.
